# Compliance Guidelines for Fundraising Appeals

Guidance for reviewing a drafted appeal letter before it is mailed. This document
governs letter *content* risk — legally required disclosure text (tax-deductibility
language, state solicitation-registration notices) is looked up separately per
donor and attached to every mailing regardless of what this guidance says.

## Donor Bill of Rights (excerpt)

Donors have the right to be told the truth about how their gift will be used, to
be treated with respect, and to be free of high-pressure tactics. A letter must
never imply that a decision to give or not give is required immediately, and must
never suggest a donor who declines is responsible for a bad outcome.

## No guarantees or overreach

Never state or imply that a single gift will completely solve a systemic
problem ("your gift will end animal homelessness"). Impact claims must be scoped
to what a specific gift amount concretely funds, matching a cited figure from
program or impact documentation.

## No tax advice

A letter may reference that gifts are tax-deductible only via the standard,
separately attached disclosure statement. It must never state a donor's specific
tax outcome, estimate their savings, or claim to speak for their tax advisor.
Tax law varies by donor; only the donor's own advisor can say what a gift means
for them.

## Honesty about need and impact

Every figure or outcome cited must come from the organization's own published
impact or program documentation. A claim with no traceable source is a
compliance risk, not just a quality one — an unsubstantiated statement in a
solicitation can expose the organization to regulatory and reputational harm,
independent of whether it was well-intentioned.

## No coercive pressure

Avoid manufactured urgency ("today only"), distressing imagery beyond what is
necessary to describe real need, or language implying the donor is at fault for
outcomes they didn't cause. Respect for capacity applies here too: never pressure
a donor beyond what their giving history shows they can sustain.

## When to flag for human review

Flag a letter rather than approving it outright when a claim can't be traced to
retrieved guidance, when the ask or tone reads as pressuring rather than
inviting, or when any tax or guarantee language appears at all. A flagged letter
is not a failure — it is the review step working as intended.
